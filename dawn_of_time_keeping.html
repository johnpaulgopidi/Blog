The Paulian Calendar: Integrating Biblical Timekeeping with Modern Astronomy

John Paul • Updated: July 9, 2025

Abstract — The Paulian Calendar is a timekeeping system developed to honor Biblical principles while incorporating precise astronomical data. It seeks optimal balance between calculated date system and the precision of eye-witness observations. Days and lunar months are calculated based on scriptural guidance alongside modern scientific methods. The system maintains alignment with the solar year over a 19-year cycle without requiring additional rule adjustments. This approach offers a thoughtful blend of faith-based timekeeping and contemporary astronomy, providing a framework for observing appointed times and exploring historical methods of tracking time. The goal of the Paulian Calendar is to look forward to the second coming of Lord Jesus Christ.

1. Introduction

1.1 God's Design in Time

The Biblical calendar is built upon the wisdom and order that God established in creation. Scripture teaches that a day begins and ends at sunset (Genesis 1:5; Leviticus 23:32), and this truth can be seen in nature, as birds and other creatures settle in before the close of the day.

Then God said, “Let there be lights in the expanse of the heavens to separate the day from the night, and they shall serve as signs and for seasons, and for days and years; and they shall serve as lights in the expanse of the heavens to give light on the earth”; and it was so.
– Genesis 1:14-15 NASB

1.2 The Exodus and the First Biblical Calendar

God first gave this calendar to the Israelites when He brought them out of Egypt (Exodus 12:1–2), calling them out of bondage into freedom. Egypt then was a slaver, just as sin enslaves humanity. But God in His mercy, sent His only begotten Son, Jesus Christ, as the perfect sacrifice to redeem mankind and to cleanse us from sin (John 1:29; Romans 5:8; Hebrews 9:12). Jesus is the true Passover Lamb, fulfilling the prophetic patterns given in the Old Testament (1 Corinthians 5:7).

1.3 Messiah and the Prophetic Timetable

Scripture reveals God’s timing and His plan to save His people. Jesus said He would be “three days and three nights in the heart of the earth” (Matthew 12:40), and He rose on the third day (1 Corinthians 15:3–4). Hosea 6:2 speaks of God’s people, saying, “After two days He will revive us; on the third day He will raise us up, so we may live in His presence.” This points to God’s promise to restore His people. The parable of the Good Samaritan also offers insight into God’s prophetic timeline for His people. In the story, the Samaritan gives the innkeeper two denarii, equal to two days’ wages, and promises to return (Luke 10:35). This can be seen as a picture of Christ caring for His people, leaving them in the care of the innkeeper (the Holy Spirit), and promising to return after a time, perhaps two days. As Scripture says, “With the Lord one day is like a thousand years, and a thousand years like one day” (2 Peter 3:8; Psalm 90:4). Nearly two thousand years have passed since Jesus’ earthly ministry, reminding us that His return to gather His people is near.

1.4 Purpose of the Paulian Calendar

The Paulian Calendar seeks to honor the rhythms God set in place and to help watch for His appointed times (Festivals). It points both backward to the deliverance God accomplished in Christ and forward to the hope of His glorious appearing, encouraging hearts to stay anchored in the promises of God’s Word.

2. Related Work

Several researchers and scholars have proposed Biblical calendars grounded in both scriptural principles and astronomical observations. The following subsections summarize key contributions and highlight distinctions relative to the Paulian Calendar.

2.1 Michael Rood's Calendar

Michael Rood has developed a calendar system closely related to the Paulian Calendar in its adherence to Biblical timekeeping. His method incorporates annual barley observations to determine the start of the Biblical year as it should supposed to be. However, the calendar reportedly starts from the time of creation, unlike Paulian Calendar which starts from the year of crucifixion of Lord Jesus and is easily accessible to critic and verify.

2.2 Abib of God

'Abib of God' website, provides extensive explanations of natural indicators, particularly surrounding the arrival of spring. Their work emphasizes environmental signs but diverges from the principle that barley should be in the Aviv state (an earliest state of Barley crop which is ready to reap) by the beginning of the first Biblical month. (This is debatable among scholars) but some Passovers mentioned in the Bible which are mandatorily celebrated on the first month of the year were the months started with Barley in the Aviv state by the first new moon day of the new year. Instead, they assume the barley must reach Aviv by the 13th day of that month.

2.3 CHC Publications

CHC Publications presents a calendar system bearing similarities to the Paulian Calendar, especially regarding the finding of new moon crescents to start the month, and is also according to the state of the Barley crop. However, CHC Publications proposes a different date for the crucifixion of the Lord Jesus Christ and employs distinct criteria for determining the first visible crescent of the new moon. Notably, CHC Publications emphasizes a strictly literal interpretation of Lord Jesus’ statement about being “three days and three nights in the heart of the earth,” leading to variations in their chronological framework with no emphasis on time keeping, beginning the crucifixion of Lord Jesus Christ. The calendar does not keep time according to the Jewish months and years but only references the Gregorian calendar.

2.4 The Modern Hebrew Calendar

The modern Hebrew calendar is different from calendars based on seeing the new crescent moon. It uses calculated times, called molad, when the moon is aligned with the sun to start each month. It also employs postponement rules (dechiyot) to shift holidays like Yom Kippur or Passover, preventing them from falling adjacent to Shabbat, ensuring ritual compatibility and addressing practical concerns like food preparation.

2.5 The Karaite Calendar

The Karaite calendar offers another distinct approach, diverging significantly from the modern Hebrew calendar. The Karaite Jewish community insists on eye-witness observation of the new crescent moon to declare the start of each month, rejecting calculated molad times. Additionally, the start of the Biblical year is determined by physical inspection of the barley (Aviv) condition in Israel, rather than relying solely on fixed intercalation cycles (leap year intervals). The Karaites also avoid postponement rules, aiming to preserve the calendar’s alignment with visible celestial signs and agricultural readiness. This commitment to observation makes the Karaite calendar more variable but reportedly the closest to ancient practices described in the Torah.

2.6 The Paulian Calendar

The Paulian Calendar seeks to integrate Biblical principles with precise astronomical data, offering several distinguishing features:

  • Biblical Timekeeping: Time is tracked according to Biblical reckoning, where days begin at sunset, and lunar cycles and solar years define the calendar structure.
  • Solar-Lunar Alignment: The calendar is designed to align naturally with solar years over a 19-year Metonic cycle, with normal cumulative drift without requiring periodic rule adjustments.
  • Astronomical Crescent Criteria: In determining new moons, the Paulian Calendar utilizes two widely accepted astronomical models: Yallop’s q-parameter method and Odeh’s criterion. This inspires from the historical practice of relying on two human witnesses for crescent visibility, while preserving the principle of verification inherent in Biblical law.
  • Time Standardization: All calculations are referenced to the UTC time zone to maintain consistency and global applicability.
  • Christ-Centered Purpose: The primary aim of the Paulian Calendar is to honor and look forward to the second coming of the Lord Jesus Christ. Drawing inspiration from the Exodus narrative, the calendar’s epoch begins with the solar year traditionally identified with the crucifixion of Christ, symbolizing deliverance and redemption.

By combining scriptural fidelity with modern astronomical precision, the Paulian Calendar seeks to provide a robust framework for Biblical observances and prophetic studies, while addressing limitations observed in prior approaches. Additionally, it seeks optimal balance between calculated date system and the precision of eye-witness observations.

3. Method

The Paulian Calendar is built using a combination of scriptural guidelines and modern astronomical calculations. This section outlines the methods employed for defining days, weeks, months, and years within the calendar.

3.1 Data Retrieval

Astronomical data are retrieved from JPL's DE441 ephemeris using the Skyfield library. DE441 provides high-precision positions of solar system bodies over an extended period, from -13,200 to +17,191 years. Calculations are performed for London, UK, in the UTC time zone.

3.2 Days

In accordance with Genesis 1:5, which declares, “And God called the light Day, and the darkness He called Night. And the evening and the morning were the first day,” a Biblical day begins at sunset and concludes at the following sunset. To honor this divine order, sunset timings are calculated daily using the Skyfield library, ensuring precise determinations based on astronomical ephemeris data.

3.3 Weeks

The Bible teaches that a week has seven days, with Sunday as the first day and the seventh day as the Sabbath, following Exodus 31:15, which says, “Six days may work be done; but in the seventh is the sabbath of rest, holy to the Lord.”

The Paulian Calendar follows this pattern and starts counting time from a Sabbath day. This starting point is based on observations from the year AD 33, which is believed to be the year when Lord Jesus was crucified and rose again.

3.4 Months

In the Bible, months are defined from new moon to new moon, as supported by scriptures such as Numbers 10:10, which states, “Also in the day of your gladness, and in your solemn days, and in the beginnings of your months, ye shall blow with the trumpets over your burnt offerings” (KJV), and 1 Samuel 20:5, where David says, “Behold, tomorrow is the new moon, and I should not fail to sit with the king at meat” (KJV). Isaiah’s prophecy for the Millennial Kingdom reinforces this, stating, “And it shall come to pass, that from one new moon to another, and from one sabbath to another, shall all flesh come to worship before me, saith the LORD” (Isaiah 66:23, KJV), indicating new moon observances in a future era of worship. Months are determined by the visibility of the new moon crescent, using Yallop's q criteria and Odeh's criterion:

  • Yallop's q Criterion: This method calculates a parameter q based on the geocentric difference in altitude between the Sun and Moon and the topocentric width of the lunar crescent. Visibility categories are: q ≥ 0.400 (very easily visible), 0.160 ≤ q < 0.400 (easily visible), -0.014 ≤ q < 0.160 (visible under perfect conditions), -0.160 ≤ q < -0.014 (optical aid may be needed), q < -0.160 (not visible).
  • Odeh's Criterion: This uses the topocentric arc of vision and crescent width, based on 737 observations, predicting visibility for naked eye and optical aid, with a Danjon limit of 6.4 degrees.

The month begins when the crescent is predicted to be visible from UTC time zone by both criterion. Months are capped to 30 days if the crescent moon is not found and next day is declared as start of the month.

3.5 Years

3.5.1 Biblical Foundations for the New Year

In the Paulian Calendar, the year starts in spring because God's word tells us to follow His appointed times, like the festivals, in their proper seasons. The Bible shows that months were often counted by the moon's cycles. But 12 lunar months are about 11 days shorter than a solar year, which is the 365.25 days it takes the Earth to travel around the sun. To keep the festivals in the right season, an extra month, a leap month is sometimes added. The Bible doesn't directly mention this leap month, but it's a practical way to make sure the appointed times (festival times) stay in order.

Then the Lord spoke to Moses, saying, ‘Speak to the sons of Israel and say to them, When you enter the land which I am going to give to you and you gather its harvest, then you shall bring in the sheaf of the first fruits of your harvest to the priest. He shall wave the sheaf before the Lord for you to be accepted; on the day after the Sabbath the priest shall wave it... Until this very day, until you have brought in the offering of your God, you shall eat neither bread nor roasted grain nor new produce. It is to be a permanent statute throughout your generations in all your dwelling places.’
– Leviticus 23:9-11,14 NASB

3.5.2 When Does the Year Begin?

God told the Israelites to celebrate the festivals at set times. For example, in the first month, during the Feast of Unleavened Bread, they were to bring a special offering of grain to the Lord after the Sabbath (Leviticus 23:9-11,14) (Antiquities of the Jews 3.10.5). God called the first month "Abib," which means "ear of grain" or "ripening grain." This shows the new year starts when the barley is ready to harvest. If it's not ripe enough, a leap month is added so the harvest and offering happen in the right season. In today's calendar, the median date of Aviv state of Barley is typically on March 22.

3.5.3 Historical Witnesses: Josephus and the Talmud

The Jewish historian Josephus confirms this practice in Antiquities of the Jews (Book 3, Chapter 10, Section 5), stating that the month of Nisan begins the year when the barley is ripe enough for the Passover offering. Talmudic writings (Sanhedrin 11b) support that the Sanhedrin checked the barley state and would insert a leap month, called Adar II, if it wasn't in the Aviv stage by the end of the 12th month, also considering the equinox to keep Passover in spring.

3.5.4 Heavenly Signs: The Lights in the Sky

Genesis 1:14–15, “Then God said, ‘Let there be lights in the expanse of the heavens to separate the day from the night, and they shall serve as signs and for seasons, and for days and years; and they shall serve as lights in the expanse of the heavens to give light on the earth’; and it was so.” teaches that God gave the sun, moon, and stars to mark times and seasons. So, the new year begins with the new moon and ripe barley, guided by these heavenly signs. That is to interpret as, for an year to start it both depends on the spring arrival (around the equinox) and barley being in the Aviv state.

3.5.5 The Year of the Lord's Crucifixion

The Paulian Calendar is built around the life of our Lord Jesus Christ, especially His crucifixion. The Gospel of John pays close attention to times and festivals tied to Jesus’ works. When Lazarus died, Jesus said there are “twelve hours in the day,” and soon after, it was Passover, which happens on the 14th day of the first month. This hints that Lazarus’ death might have been near the new year.

So when He heard that he was sick, He then stayed two days longer in the place where He was. Then after this He said to the disciples, ‘Let’s go to Judea again.’ The disciples said to Him, ‘Rabbi, the Jews were just now seeking to stone You, and yet You are going there again?’ Jesus replied, ‘Are there not twelve hours in the day? If anyone walks during the day, he does not stumble, because he sees the light of this world. But if anyone walks during the night, he stumbles, because the light is not in him.’... So when Jesus came, He found that he had already been in the tomb four days.
– John 11:6-10,17 NASB

In Israel, day and night are equal around March 16 or 17, called the “equilux.” If Jesus spoke these words a couple of days before reaching Lazarus, it might have been around March 18 when He raised Lazarus from the dead.

3.5.6 Dating the Crucifixion

For just as Jonah was in the stomach of the sea monster for three days and three nights, so will the Son of Man be in the heart of the earth for three days and three nights.
– Matthew 12:40 NASB

There is wide consensus that Lord Jesus was crucified in 33 AD, and this year marks the start of the Paulian Calendar. Several reasons support this date:

  • Day of the Week: Gospel accounts show Jesus died before the Sabbath, suggesting a Friday crucifixion. In 33 AD, Passover (Nisan 14) likely fell on a Thursday evening to Friday.
  • Astronomy: A partial lunar eclipse was visible in Jerusalem on April 3, 33 AD (Acts 2:20), aligning with the crucifixion.
  • Idiomatic Interpretation: The phrase “three days and three nights” in Matthew 12:40 is often understood as a Jewish idiom, not a literal 72-hour period. In Jewish tradition, part of a day could count as a whole day. Other scriptures confirm Jesus rose “on the third day”:
So it is written, that the Christ would suffer and rise from the dead on the third day.
– Luke 24:46 NASB
And that He was buried, and that He was raised on the third day according to the Scriptures.
– 1 Corinthians 15:4 NASB
He will revive us after two days; He will raise us up on the third day, that we may live before Him.
– Hosea 6:2 NASB

These verses show the resurrection “on the third day” after parts of three days: Friday (day 1), Saturday (day 2), and Sunday (day 3). This fits the timeline of 33 AD, where Jesus died Friday afternoon, remained in the tomb through Saturday, and rose early Sunday.

Historical Context: 33 AD aligns with Pontius Pilate's governorship and Lord Jesus's 3-year ministry.

3.5.7 Why 33 AD Over 30 AD?

Some suggest 30 AD as a possible year for the crucifixion because astronomical calculations indicate Nisan 14 may have fallen on a Friday, April 7, aligning with the Gospel accounts of a Friday crucifixion. However, 33 AD is more likely for several reasons. First, the lunar eclipse on April 3, 33 AD, visible in Jerusalem, matches the prophecy in Acts 2:20, while no such eclipse occurred in 30 AD. Finally, the close alignment of the new moon, the vernal equinox, and the barley harvest in 33 AD strongly supports Nisan 14 falling on April 3, a Friday, fulfilling both Biblical and astronomical criteria. God’s perfect timing is seen in how these events fulfill prophecy, with Jesus’ death and resurrection marking the heart of the Paulian Calendar.

3.5.8 Intercalation Rule in the Paulian Calendar

To keep the festivals in the proper seasons, the Paulian Calendar uses a fixed rule:

If the end of the 12th lunar month falls before March 18, insert a 13th month; otherwise, begin the new year.

Why March 18?

It follows the equi-lux period (March 16-17) and precedes the typical barley ripening window (around March 22). Using March 18, the calendar seeks optimal balance between agricultural, scriptural, and astronomical factors, taking the inspiration from 33 AD (the year of Lord's crucifixion)

Why not the “first new moon after the equinox”?

It can push Passover well into late spring or early summer contrary to the Biblical emphasis that Passover and Firstfruits remain anchored in spring, when the barley truly ripens.

5. Discussion

The Paulian Calendar seeks to represent a unique integration of Biblical timekeeping principles with modern astronomical precision. Its key features include tracking time Biblically with sunsets defining days, lunar cycles for months, and years aligned with agricultural cycles. Unlike the modern Hebrew calendar, which relies on calculated molad and postponement rules, the Paulian Calendar prioritizes observable celestial signs, such as the new moon crescent, echoing the Karaite approach but with Yallop’s and Odeh’s criteria for crescent visibility.

6. Conclusions

The Paulian Calendar seeks to bring together the rhythms of Scripture and the exactness of modern astronomy, creating a dependable system both for faithful observance and for rigorous historical research. Future developments aim to integrate real-time observational data and leverage artificial intelligence to enhance both predictive accuracy and usability.
